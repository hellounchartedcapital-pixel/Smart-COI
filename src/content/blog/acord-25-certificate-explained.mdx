## What Is the ACORD 25 Form?

The ACORD 25 is the standard Certificate of Liability Insurance form used across the United States insurance industry. ACORD (Association for Cooperative Operations Research and Development) is the nonprofit organization that develops standardized forms for the insurance industry. The ACORD 25 form is by far the most common certificate format you will encounter as a property manager.

When a vendor, contractor, or tenant provides you with a "certificate of insurance" or "COI," they are almost always providing an ACORD 25 form (or occasionally an [ACORD 28](/blog/acord-28-evidence-of-property-insurance) for property insurance). Understanding this form is essential for anyone responsible for verifying insurance compliance. For a broader overview of what compliance tracking involves, see our [complete COI compliance guide](/blog/coi-compliance-guide-property-managers).

The ACORD 25 is a one-page summary document. It does not grant coverage — it merely summarizes the policies in effect at the time the certificate was issued. This distinction matters because policies can be modified, cancelled, or allowed to lapse after the certificate is generated.

## Why Property Managers Need to Understand ACORD 25 Forms

If you manage commercial properties, you review ACORD 25 certificates regularly — whether you realize it or not. Every vendor, contractor, and tenant who provides proof of insurance is submitting some version of this form. Understanding its structure means you can quickly identify coverage gaps, catch expired policies, and verify that your organization is properly protected.

The consequences of misreading an ACORD 25 are real. If a contractor's general liability policy expired two weeks before a slip-and-fall on your property, and nobody caught it, your organization may be on the hook for the full claim. If a vendor's certificate lists your company as the certificate holder but not as an additional insured, you may have no coverage rights at all — despite believing you were protected.

Property managers who understand this form can review certificates in minutes rather than hours, ask the right questions when something looks off, and set accurate compliance requirements from the start. For teams managing dozens or hundreds of vendor relationships, this knowledge pays for itself many times over.

## The Header Section

The top of the ACORD 25 form contains several important fields.

**Certificate date.** The date the certificate was issued. This is not the policy effective date — it is when the document was generated. A recently issued certificate is more reliable than one issued months ago because it reflects the policy status at the time of issuance. As a best practice, request certificates that were issued within the last 30 days.

**Certificate number.** An identifier assigned by the producer's certificate management system. This number is useful for tracking reissues and updates. If a vendor sends a revised certificate, the certificate number may change, helping you identify which version is current.

**Producer.** The insurance agent or broker who issued the certificate. The producer is the vendor's agent, not yours. If you need to verify information or request corrections, you may need to contact the producer — but the vendor should be your primary point of contact for certificate issues.

**Important note:** The certificate itself includes a disclaimer stating that it is issued as a matter of information only and confers no rights upon the certificate holder. This legal language reinforces that the ACORD 25 is a summary, not a contract of insurance. The actual policy documents govern coverage.

## The Insured Section

This section identifies the named insured — the entity that purchased the insurance policies. For COI verification purposes, this is the vendor or tenant whose compliance you are checking.

Verify that the named insured matches the entity in your contract or lease. If your agreement is with "ABC Contracting LLC" but the certificate shows "ABC Contracting, Inc." as the named insured, there may be a corporate structure issue that warrants investigation. The named insured should be the legal entity you have a contractual relationship with.

## Insurance Companies (Insurers)

The form lists up to five insurance companies (labeled Insurer A through Insurer E) that provide coverage. Each insurer is identified by name and a NAIC (National Association of Insurance Commissioners) number. The NAIC number can be used to verify that the insurer is legitimate and licensed in the relevant state.

Different coverage types may be provided by different insurers. It is common for a vendor to have general liability with one carrier, workers' compensation with another, and automobile coverage with a third. The letter designation (A, B, C, etc.) connects each insurer to the coverage types listed below.

## Coverage Section: General Liability

The general liability section is typically the largest and most important part of the ACORD 25 for property managers. Here is what each field means.

**Type of insurance.** Most vendors carry "Commercial General Liability" (CGL). Look for checkboxes indicating the policy type — occurrence form vs claims-made form. Occurrence form is standard and preferred because it covers incidents that occur during the policy period regardless of when the claim is filed. Claims-made policies only cover claims filed during the policy period.

**Policy number.** The unique identifier for the general liability policy.

**Policy effective date and expiration date.** The period during which the policy is active. Both dates should be verified — the policy should be currently in effect and the expiration date should extend through the period when the vendor will be performing work.

**Limits.** The general liability section lists several limit types:

- **Each Occurrence:** The maximum the insurer will pay for a single incident. This is the limit most commonly specified in contracts. Typical requirements range from $500,000 to $2,000,000 per occurrence.
- **Damage to Rented Premises:** Coverage for damage to premises rented to the insured. Relevant for tenants.
- **Medical Expense:** Coverage for minor medical expenses regardless of fault. Usually a small amount ($5,000-$10,000).
- **Personal & Advertising Injury:** Coverage for claims of libel, slander, false arrest, and similar offenses.
- **General Aggregate:** The total maximum the insurer will pay for all claims during the policy period (except products/completed operations). This is typically twice the per-occurrence limit.
- **Products/Completed Operations Aggregate:** A separate aggregate for claims arising from the insured's products or completed work.

When checking compliance, focus on the **Each Occurrence** and **General Aggregate** limits, as these are most commonly specified in contracts and leases.

## Coverage Section: Automobile Liability

If the vendor operates vehicles on or near your property, automobile liability coverage is important.

**Combined Single Limit (CSL).** The maximum the insurer will pay for a single auto accident, combining bodily injury and property damage. Common requirements are $500,000 to $1,000,000 CSL.

**Type checkboxes.** Look for "Any Auto" which provides the broadest coverage. "Owned Autos Only" or "Hired Autos" provide narrower coverage that may not be sufficient.

## Coverage Section: Workers' Compensation

Workers' compensation coverage is required by law in most states for employers. The certificate should show the statutory limits for the applicable state.

**WC Statutory Limits.** The checkbox indicating that the policy meets the state's statutory requirements. This should be checked.

**Employers' Liability limits.** Additional coverage beyond statutory workers' comp:
- **Each Accident:** Per-accident limit, typically $500,000 to $1,000,000.
- **Disease - Each Employee:** Per-employee disease limit.
- **Disease - Policy Limit:** Aggregate disease limit.

If a vendor's employee is injured on your property and the vendor does not have workers' comp coverage, the injured worker may file a claim against your organization. Always verify this coverage is current.

## Coverage Section: Umbrella / Excess Liability

Umbrella or excess liability provides additional coverage above the limits of underlying policies (general liability, auto, workers' comp). Contracts for higher-risk work often require umbrella coverage.

**Each Occurrence and Aggregate.** These limits sit on top of the underlying policy limits. For example, if the vendor has $1M per occurrence in general liability and $5M in umbrella coverage, the effective per-occurrence limit is $6M.

## Description of Operations / Locations / Vehicles

This is the free-text section at the bottom-center of the form and is one of the most important sections for property managers to review. It contains information that does not fit into the structured fields above, including:

**Additional insured entities.** This is where you will most often find confirmation that your organization is listed as additional insured. Look for your exact entity name and the endorsement form number (e.g., CG 20 10, CG 20 26).

**Waiver of subrogation.** Notation that the vendor's policies include a waiver of subrogation in favor of your entities. Learn more about [waiver of subrogation for property managers](/blog/waiver-of-subrogation-property-managers).

**Primary and noncontributory.** Language indicating that the vendor's insurance is primary and will not seek contribution from your own policies.

**Project or location specific information.** Some certificates specify the property address or project name to which the coverage applies.

Read this section carefully. It is free-form text, so formatting varies widely between brokers. Critical information is sometimes buried in dense paragraphs. ## Certificate Holder Section

The certificate holder section at the very bottom of the form identifies who requested the certificate and where it should be mailed. **Being listed as the certificate holder does not make you an [additional insured](/blog/what-is-additional-insured-commercial-real-estate).** This is the single most common misconception in COI review.

The certificate holder simply receives a copy of the certificate. It grants no insurance rights whatsoever. Do not confuse certificate holder status with additional insured status — they are completely different.

Verify that your organization's name and address are correct in this section, but understand that the real coverage rights come from the additional insured endorsement, not the certificate holder designation.

## ACORD 25 vs. ACORD 28: What's the Difference?

These two forms are the backbone of insurance verification in commercial real estate, but they serve very different purposes.

**ACORD 25 — Certificate of Liability Insurance.** This form summarizes liability policies: commercial general liability, automobile liability, umbrella/excess liability, and workers' compensation. It answers the question: "If someone gets hurt or something gets damaged because of this vendor's work, is there coverage?"

**ACORD 28 — Evidence of Commercial Property Insurance.** This form summarizes property insurance: building coverage, business personal property, equipment breakdown, and related coverages. It answers the question: "If the tenant's space or contents are damaged by fire, flood, or another peril, is there coverage?" Read our full guide to the [ACORD 28 form](/blog/acord-28-evidence-of-property-insurance).

**When to request which form:**

- **Vendors and contractors:** Request the ACORD 25. You need to verify their liability coverage in case their work causes injury or property damage at your building.
- **Tenants:** Request both. The ACORD 25 verifies their liability coverage (slip-and-fall in their space, for example), and the ACORD 28 verifies they carry adequate property insurance on their leased space and contents.
- **Subcontractors:** Request the ACORD 25. The general contractor should ensure their subs carry adequate liability coverage, but as the property owner, you may want to verify this directly.

Most [COI tracking systems](/certificate-of-insurance-tracking) handle both form types, but the ACORD 25 is by far the more common — you will review it far more frequently than the ACORD 28.

## What Property Managers Need to Check on Every ACORD 25

Before filing away any certificate, run through these verification points. Missing even one can leave your property exposed.

**1. Coverage types match your requirements.** Confirm every required coverage type is present — general liability, automobile liability, workers' compensation, and umbrella/excess if required. A vendor with excellent GL coverage but no workers' comp still has a critical gap. For guidance on setting the right requirements, see [how to set insurance requirements for commercial real estate](/blog/how-to-set-insurance-requirements-commercial-real-estate).

**2. Limits meet or exceed your minimums.** Do not just confirm that coverage exists — verify the actual dollar amounts. A $300,000 per-occurrence limit does not satisfy a $1,000,000 requirement. Compare both the per-occurrence and general aggregate limits against your standards.

**3. Policy dates are current.** Check every policy's effective and expiration dates. If any policy has expired or expires before the vendor's work period ends, the certificate is inadequate. Be especially careful with certificates issued months ago — the underlying policies may have lapsed since issuance. For more on this, see [COI expiration tracking best practices](/blog/coi-expiration-tracking-best-practices).

**4. Your entity is listed as [Additional Insured](/blog/what-is-additional-insured-commercial-real-estate).** Look in the Description of Operations section for your exact legal entity name and an endorsement form reference (CG 20 10, CG 20 26, or CG 20 37). Being listed as certificate holder alone provides zero coverage rights.

**5. Your entity is listed as Certificate Holder with correct address.** While this is administrative rather than a coverage right, an incorrect certificate holder name or address can signal sloppy paperwork — and if the name is wrong here, it may be wrong on the endorsement too.

**6. Endorsement forms are referenced.** Look for specific form numbers in the Description of Operations: CG 20 10 (Additional Insured — Owners, Lessees or Contractors), CG 20 37 (Additional Insured — Owners, Lessees or Contractors — Completed Operations), and Waiver of Subrogation language.

**7. Property address appears in Description of Operations.** For location-specific coverage, the property address or project name should be referenced. Without it, there may be ambiguity about which location the coverage applies to.

**8. Carrier has an acceptable AM Best rating.** The NAIC numbers on the form can be used to look up each carrier's financial strength rating. An insurer with a poor rating may not be able to pay claims when they arise.

## Common ACORD 25 Mistakes That Create Liability Gaps

### Wrong entity name as Additional Insured

Entity name precision matters. If your ownership entity is "Westfield Properties LP" but the endorsement lists "Westfield Properties LLC," the coverage may not apply to your entity. LLC, LP, Inc., and Corp. are different legal structures. Insist on the exact legal name that appears in your contracts and operating agreements.

### Certificate Holder listed but not Additional Insured

This is the single most common and most dangerous mistake. Being named as the certificate holder is an administrative designation — it means you receive a copy of the certificate. It grants absolutely no insurance coverage rights. [Additional Insured status](/blog/what-is-additional-insured-commercial-real-estate) is a separate endorsement that extends the vendor's coverage to protect you. These are completely different things, and confusing them can leave you fully exposed to claims.

### Expired policy dates on a recently issued certificate

A certificate generated today can still show expired policies. The certificate date (when the document was created) and the policy dates (when coverage is active) are independent fields. Always verify that every listed policy is currently in effect — do not assume that a recently issued certificate means the underlying policies are current.

### Missing Waiver of Subrogation endorsement

If your contract requires Waiver of Subrogation but the certificate does not reference it in the Description of Operations, you are exposed. Without this endorsement, the vendor's insurer could pay a claim and then sue your organization to recover the payment. The waiver prevents this. Learn more in our guide to [Waiver of Subrogation for property managers](/blog/waiver-of-subrogation-property-managers).

### "Blanket" additional insured without an endorsement form reference

Some certificates state "blanket additional insured" in the Description of Operations but do not reference a specific endorsement form number (such as CG 20 10). Without the actual endorsement form, "blanket" language may not provide enforceable coverage. Always look for the specific CG form numbers.

### Umbrella/excess not following form over underlying policies

If your requirements include umbrella coverage, verify that the umbrella policy "follows form" over the underlying general liability and auto policies. An umbrella policy with exclusions or limitations that do not align with the underlying coverage may create gaps at the higher limit tiers. The umbrella section should reference the same coverage types as the underlying policies.

### Not checking all coverage types independently

Reviewing only general liability and ignoring workers' comp, auto, or umbrella coverage is a common shortcut that creates risk. Each coverage type should be verified independently against your requirements. A vendor with excellent GL coverage but no workers' compensation presents a serious liability gap.

### Ignoring the policy form type

Occurrence form and claims-made form are not interchangeable. Occurrence form covers incidents that happen during the policy period regardless of when the claim is filed. Claims-made form only covers claims actually filed during the policy period. If your contract requires occurrence form, a claims-made policy may not satisfy the requirement.

## A Quick ACORD 25 Review Checklist

When you receive an ACORD 25 certificate, use this checklist to verify the essentials:

1. **Named insured** — Does it match the entity in your contract or lease?
2. **Certificate date** — Was the certificate issued recently (within the last 30 days)?
3. **Policy dates** — Are all listed policies currently in effect?
4. **General liability limits** — Do the per-occurrence and general aggregate limits meet your requirements?
5. **Workers' compensation** — Is the WC Statutory Limits box checked?
6. **Auto liability** — If applicable, does the combined single limit meet your threshold?
7. **Umbrella/excess** — If required, is umbrella coverage listed with adequate limits?
8. **Additional insured** — Is your organization named in the Description of Operations section?
9. **Waiver of subrogation** — If required, is it noted in the Description of Operations?
10. **Certificate holder** — Is your organization correctly listed at the bottom of the form?

This takes 2-3 minutes per certificate when done manually. Across a portfolio of 50+ vendors, that adds up to hours of review time each month — which is why many property managers are turning to automated solutions.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**What is an ACORD 25 form?**

An ACORD 25 is a standardized certificate of liability insurance used to provide proof of insurance coverage. It summarizes a company's active insurance policies including general liability, automobile liability, umbrella/excess liability, and workers' compensation. The form is issued by the insured party's insurance agent or broker and is the most common certificate format in the United States.

**What is the difference between ACORD 25 and ACORD 28?**

ACORD 25 covers liability insurance — general liability, automobile liability, workers' compensation, and umbrella/excess liability. [ACORD 28](/blog/acord-28-evidence-of-property-insurance) covers commercial property insurance — building coverage, business personal property, and related perils. Property managers typically need the ACORD 25 from vendors and contractors, and both the ACORD 25 and ACORD 28 from tenants.

**Who fills out an ACORD 25?**

The insurance agent or broker fills out the ACORD 25 on behalf of the insured party. The insured (vendor, contractor, or tenant) requests the certificate from their agent, who then issues it to the requesting party — typically the property manager or building owner who requires proof of insurance.

**Does an ACORD 25 expire?**

The certificate itself does not technically expire, but the insurance policies listed on it have specific expiration dates. Once any listed policy expires, the certificate no longer provides evidence of current coverage and a new certificate should be requested. This is why [tracking COI expirations](/blog/coi-expiration-tracking-best-practices) is a critical part of compliance management.

**What should a certificate holder look for on an ACORD 25?**

Verify that coverage types and limits meet your requirements, all policy dates are current, your entity is named as both Certificate Holder and [Additional Insured](/blog/what-is-additional-insured-commercial-real-estate), required endorsements (CG 20 10, CG 20 37, Waiver of Subrogation) are referenced in the Description of Operations, and the property address appears. ## Key Takeaways

The ACORD 25 is a standardized but information-dense document. For property managers, the most critical elements to verify are:

- The **named insured** matches your contractual counterparty
- All required **coverage types** are present with adequate **limits**
- Your organization is listed as an **additional insured** (not just the certificate holder)
- All policies are **currently in effect** with expiration dates that cover your needs
- Any required endorsements (**waiver of subrogation**, **primary and noncontributory**) appear in the Description of Operations

Whether you review certificates manually or use AI-powered tools, knowing what each section contains and where to find critical information ensures that your compliance verification is thorough and effective. For property management teams handling ongoing COI compliance across portfolios, understanding the ACORD 25 form structure is foundational knowledge that protects your properties, your vendors, and your organization.
